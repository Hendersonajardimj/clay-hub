## The Recording

I captured this at Canal Street station around 2am. The subway platform has this constant low hum from the ventilation system, mixed with occasional distant train rumbles. I used a contact mic attached to one of the support columns to pick up the vibrations.

**Raw recording**: 30 seconds, mono, 48kHz

## Processing Chain

### 1. Initial Cleanup

First, I trim the recording to the cleanest 10-second loop and apply a subtle high-pass filter at 80Hz to remove unnecessary sub-bass rumble that muddies the mix.

```
EQ Eight:
- High-pass at 80Hz (12dB/oct)
- Slight boost at 200Hz (+2dB) to warm it up
```

### 2. Grain Delay Magic

This is where the texture comes alive. Ableton's Grain Delay is perfect for turning static recordings into evolving pads.

```
Grain Delay:
- Spray: 45ms
- Frequency: 0.35Hz
- Pitch: -7 semitones
- Dry/Wet: 70%
```

The negative pitch shift drops the subway hum into a pad register, while the spray parameter adds movement.

### 3. Reverb Layer

I send the grain delay output to a reverb return track to create depth.

```
Valhalla VintageVerb:
- Decay: 8 seconds
- Mix: 40%
- Pre-delay: 20ms
- Color: Dark
```

### 4. Final Polish

A compressor with slow attack to glue everything together, plus a bit of saturation for warmth.

## Result

What started as a noisy subway recording becomes a rich, evolving pad perfect for ambient breaks or transitions. The texture retains that "urban" quality but sits beautifully in a mix.

## Try This Yourself

Go out with a contact mic or even just your phone. Find structures that hum: HVAC units, bridges, metal railings. The key is finding sounds with consistent frequency content that grain delay can grab onto.
